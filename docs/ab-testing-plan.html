The core hypothesis: CTR is limited not by email open rate (open rate is improving) but by product-grid relevance. A subscriber opens, scans the product grid, finds nothing that matches their size/color/intent, and does not click. Both tests attack this problem from different angles: Test 1 adds more products (breadth), Test 2 adds variant detail (specificity).

Test 1 attacks: Product breadth

The subscriber opens the email and none of the 6 products match their current intent. Adding 2 more products (8 total, 4 balanced rows) increases the probability of a match. 8 products maintains grid symmetry — unlike the failed 7-product layout which left an orphaned bottom row.

Test 2 attacks: Product specificity

The subscriber sees a product they like but hesitates because they don't know if it comes in their color or size. Showing color swatches and size pills directly inside the product block removes this friction. The click on a specific variant swatch is already pre-qualified intent.

Test 1 — 8 Products vs 6 Products
Adding a 4th product row (Products 7–8) to the email body. Product count goes from 6 to 8 while maintaining the 2-up grid and square image format. All other email elements (hero, body copy, footer) remain identical between control and variant.

Test 1A — Hypothesis & Layout Wireframes

CONTROL vs VARIANT

Hypothesis — Why 8 Products Will Win

  • 4 complete rows (2×4) = perfect symmetry — eliminates the orphaned-row failure seen in 7-product layouts
  • 2 additional products increases the probability that a given subscriber finds something relevant
  • GentsLux has 3 product segments (71 Tops, 72 Bottoms, 73 Others) — 8 products allows ~2–3 SKUs per segment without crowding
  • Products 7–8 can be used as price anchors or discovery products — introducing new SKUs without disrupting hero positioning
  • Longer email scroll is acceptable on mobile when product density per row stays at 2 (not 3)

Risk Factors to Monitor

  • Total email height increases ~25–30% — mobile users may not scroll to products 7–8 at all
  • Click share may cannibalize from hero products 1–2 if products 7–8 are poorly chosen
  • Content creators must source 2 additional products per send — adds briefing overhead
  • Product imagery for slots 7–8 must be prepared — adds 2 images to the design workflow per brand
  • If total clicks stay flat (subscribers click once regardless of count), CTR won't improve despite longer email

Test 1B — Product Slot Hierarchy

For Content Creators

Each slot position has a defined role. Products must be assigned to slots based on their proven conversion weight — NOT by personal preference or inventory push. Slots 7–8 are the new addition in Variant B only.

SLOTS 1–2
Hero Products
Must be the brand's highest-converting proven products. Never rotate new or untested products into these positions during an AB test.
SLOTS 3–4
Strong Secondaries
Established products with solid click history. Different category from hero if possible — gives breadth without competing with slot 1–2 directly.
SLOTS 5–6
Support Products
Good but not top performers. Can be segment-specific SKUs targeting a subsegment of the list. Include at least one price-point anchor here.
SLOTS 7–8 ★ NEW
Discovery Products
Variant B only. Use for: (1) new arrivals, (2) different price tier, or (3) complementary category. These should be DIFFERENT from slots 1–6 in some meaningful way — color, category, or price point.

Redesigning each product cell from a compact square-image block into a taller portrait-image block that shows color swatches and size pills directly inside the product card. Product count stays at 6 (control). Variant information appears only inside the product block — not in the body copy or anywhere else in the email.

Test 2A — Hypothesis & Cell Wireframes

CONTROL vs VARIANT

Hypothesis — Why Long Block + Variants Will Win

  • Color swatches tell the subscriber their preferred color is available before they click — reduces the "click to discover, leave when wrong color" pattern
  • Size pills (S/M/L/XL) confirm fit is available — especially powerful for bras (BG) and fitted clothing (LF, GL)
  • Portrait-format images (282×400px) show full-length garments — pants, dresses, and bras all look better in portrait than square crop
  • Each color swatch is a clickable link pre-filtered to that color on the product page — creates additional click paths per product without additional products
  • Apparel industry data: showing variant swatches in product grids lifts click-to-cart 8–15% by reducing friction

Risk Factors to Monitor

  • Taller cells mean each email is significantly longer — may hurt total scroll completion on mobile
  • Variant data (colors, sizes) must be sourced and kept accurate per send — incorrect variant display destroys trust
  • Color swatch circles are 14–18px — too small to render clearly on some older email clients
  • If a product has only 1 color or 1 size, variant display adds visual clutter with no benefit — apply variants only when 2+ colors or 3+ sizes exist
  • Requires coordination between content team and product/data team to pull accurate variant data per send

Test 2C — Variant Data Guide

For Content Creators + Designers
Variant data (colors, sizes) must be sourced from the product catalog before each send. This is a new workflow step. Accuracy is critical — showing a color that doesn't exist on the product page destroys trust and increases returns.
Accuracy rule: If you cannot confirm that a color or size is currently in stock for a product, do NOT show it in the swatch or size row. It is better to show fewer variants accurately than to show more variants inaccurately. Out-of-stock variants shown in the email = trust failure.

Measurement & Success Criteria

KPIs + Decision Rules
CTR
Primary KPI
Clicks ÷ Delivered (use Access/Delivered, not Access/Open — MPP-immune). Win threshold: Variant beats Control by ≥10% relative AND the difference holds across all 3 test sends.
CBH/1K
Revenue KPI
Revenue per 1,000 delivered. A CTR lift that does not translate to CBH/1K improvement means low-quality clicks — evaluate before scaling the winner.
Click Map
Distribution KPI
Where do clicks go in each variant? If 8-product email concentrates 80%+ of clicks on products 1–2 still, the additional products are not contributing. Track per-slot click count.
