T  ·  Task

Root Causes & Required Actions

Root Cause 1 — List scale-up with low-engagement addresses:
SF scaled 3× within March (Mar 5: 145K → Mar 26: 436K delivered). CTR dropped from 1.01% on Mar 5 to 0.54% on Mar 26 as less-engaged addresses dominated the later sends. LF had the same pattern (140K → 335K within March, CTR 0.91% → 0.72%). CTR recovered for LF in April when list growth was controlled, confirming list quality as a direct lever.

Root Cause 2 — Body copy fatigue (repetitive narrative pattern):
The email body follows the same structural arc across nearly every send: micro-story opener → product mention → social proof line → CTA. Subscribers who receive 4+ emails per month recognise the template and stop reading past the opener. Evidence: GL used "Customer Reviews + Thank You" as content in 3 of 4 May sends; BG's Daisy Bra appeared in almost every send Jan–May 2026. LF's CTR recovered to 1.09% in April specifically when content rotated to an anniversary theme ("Happy Birthday LuxFitting turns 3") — then fell back to 0.80% in May when the template reverted to standard formula. BG's open rate is healthy (45.2%) but CTR keeps declining — subscribers open the email, recognise the pattern, and leave without clicking.

Root Cause 3 — Hook coherence breaks under list expansion:
When sending to a larger, colder audience, the subject line hook must work for someone who barely remembers the brand. Weak or generic suspended-loop hooks lose cold subscribers in the first 200px. A hook that relies on familiarity ("you deserve this") fails for addresses that joined in the last 30 days and have no emotional history with the brand.

  • Rotate body copy narrative arc every send — minimum 2 distinct openers per month: story-led, fact-led, or question-led; avoid "Customer Reviews + Thank You" as the only template in a month
  • Enforce the suspended-loop hook formula across subject → preheader → banner → body — one theme, no competing angles; the hook must land within the first 200px for cold subscribers
  • For SF: campaigns paused through Oct 2026; relaunch in November with Christmas/Holiday Gifting angle targeting lifecycle tier 1-D VIP first, then 1-A Active
  • Monitor within-month CTR across sends — if Send 4 CTR drops >15% vs Send 1, pause list scale-up and refresh creative template before continuing
  • Maintain hero product anchors (GL: Icy Shorts + JettJeans3; BG: Daisy Bra + Posy Bra; LF: StretchActive + LinenGlam) but vary the product narrative angle each send — same product, different pain/relief story

Part 3 — Content Creation Dos & Don'ts

5 Layers · Copy · Photos · Brief
Every email creative passes through 5 layers: Input → Angle → Framework → Flow → Brief. A break at any layer causes the AI prompt to produce off-brief copy. Use this section as a pre-flight checklist before running any prompt from Part 2.
Layer 1
INPUT
What we know
Segment data · brand brief · product · season · last-send CTR
Layer 2
ANGLE
Why they care
Single emotional lens — commit before writing one word
Layer 3
FRAMEWORK
In what order
PAS / BAB / 4U — the message choreography
Layer 4
FLOW
How it unfolds
F-pattern · zone map · banner → body → CTA sequence
Layer 5
BRIEF
Execution spec
Exact copy · visual direction · product slugs · prompt input

Each layer is the output of the previous and the input of the next. A broken link → the brief fails, the AI prompt drifts, the copy misses.

L1

INPUT — What We Collect Before Writing

Segment ID · brand persona · hero product + slug · campaign theme · season trigger · last-send performance

✓ Do

  • Map the target segment's specific pain + AOV + risk profile before opening the prompt
  • Pull 2–3 exact customer review phrases that name the pain in customer language
  • Lock the single campaign theme in one sentence before writing anything
  • Note the last send's hero product and CTR — AI needs this context to avoid repetition
  • Confirm the hero product slug is lowercase and exact (used for UTM links in copy)

✗ Don't

  • Don't start writing copy without documenting input — vague input = vague output
  • Don't combine two promotions in one brief (Flash Sale + New Arrival = competing angles)
  • Don't skip segment mapping — generic copy sent to a high-risk segment drives 2%+ optout
  • Don't list 4 "hero" products — maximum 1 hero, 2 supporting products per send
  • Don't use approximate product names in the brief (always verify exact slug from the store)
L2

ANGLE — The Single Emotional Lens

Choose one approach and commit. The angle must carry through subject line → preheader → banner → body → CTA without breaking.

Angle TypeBest forAvoid when
Pain-First — open with the specific frictionCold/new subscribers; high-risk segmentsVIP or high-freq buyers (feels patronising)
Desire-First — open with the emotional outcomeVIP, high-freq, lifecycle tier 1-DCold list — desire needs brand trust first
Occasion/Trigger — tie to a season or eventMother's Day, Summer, Anniversary sendsOff-season sends without a real occasion
Social Proof — lead with customer voiceWavering mid-funnel subscribersOpening send of a new segment (no trust yet)
Product Mechanism — the specific feature that differsNew product launches; mechanism-driven USPLifestyle brands where feeling > function
Identity/Tribe — speak to who they see themselves asHigh-loyalty segments (BG 21, GL 71)Transactional or clearance sends

✓ Do

  • Choose ONE angle — write it in a single sentence before opening any prompt
  • Verify the angle matches the last-send CTR context (don't repeat a failed angle)
  • Use Pain-First for any send to a list scaled up by 20%+ that month
  • Rotate angle approach across sends — GL's CTR growth came from angle variation
  • Lock the angle before writing the subject line — the subject is the angle's opening line

✗ Don't

  • Don't write the subject line before the angle is confirmed — subject drift creates incoherence
  • Don't mix "flash sale urgency" with "educational" copy in one send — pick one tone
  • Don't reuse the same angle two sends in a row — this is the primary body copy fatigue driver
  • Don't use an Identity angle for lapsed subscribers — they've disengaged from the tribe already
  • Don't write "value-oriented" and "aspirational" simultaneously — they pull in opposite directions
L3

FRAMEWORK — The Message Choreography

How to order the argument. For email body: PAS or BAB. For subject lines: 4U. Never default to the same framework every send.

FrameworkStructureBest fit for email
PASProblem → Agitate → SolutionCold/new subscribers; re-engagement; Pain-First angle
BABBefore → After → BridgeLifecycle re-engagement; lapsed (1-C) subscribers; Desire-First angle
Social Proof + CTAVoice of customer → micro-proof → CTAVIP, high-freq buyers; retargeting sends
4U (subject lines)Useful · Urgent · Unique · Ultra-specificEvery subject line — use as a checklist, not a structure
3 Reasons WhyReason 1 → 2 → 3 → CTAProduct launch; new segment introduction only (narrative preferred)

✓ Do

  • Lead every body paragraph with the problem first — pain precedes product name
  • Agitate with specificity: not "discomfort" but "shoulder marks by noon"
  • Introduce the product as the relief mechanism — name it AFTER the pain, not before
  • Use PAS for sends to scaled lists or any new/cold subscriber segment
  • Use BAB when the subscriber has already bought once and is being re-engaged after 60+ days

✗ Don't

  • Don't use bullet lists in the body copy — narrative structure only (bullets break the persona voice)
  • Don't introduce a new angle in paragraph 3 — hook coherence rule: one theme, end to end
  • Don't start the email body with the product name or a price — the pain must come first
  • Don't use the same framework as the previous send without a reason
  • Don't use "3 Reasons Why" unless it's a product launch — it reads as an ad, not a friend

  • Price/offer must appear in Zone 1 — not only buried in the body or product row

✗ Don't

  • Don't use bullet lists or checkmarks anywhere in the email body (kills persona voice)
  • Don't open Zone 2 with "I hope this email finds you well" or any generic greeting
  • Don't skip the named micro-story — it's the primary CTR driver in BG/LF/GL email data
  • Don't put social proof in Zone 5 — by then the subscriber has already decided whether to click

Visual Reference

Photography & Visual Dos & Don'ts

Banner photography and product block images are the first visual signal the subscriber sees after opening. Misaligned visuals break the hook before the copy is read.

🖼 Banner Photography

✓ Do
  • Model 3/4 profile or from behind — avoids direct eye contact (which feels formal/ad-like in scroll)
  • Natural or diffused light — overcast/golden hour for warmth; never harsh ring-light glare on skin
  • Contextual background — bedroom, bathroom, living room; signals real life, not a studio
  • Product clearly visible in the first 200px — if the subscriber can't identify the product in 1 second, it fails the hook
  • Rotate model representation across sends to reflect audience diversity
  • Max 3 dominant colors in the composition — visual simplicity drives attention to the product
✗ Don't
  • No studio-white backgrounds — they signal "this is an ad" and reduce the friend-email feel
  • No models making direct eye contact with camera — confrontational, increases scroll-through rate
  • No more than 3 colors in the banner frame — visual clutter kills the hook in the F-pattern scan
  • Don't use a banner where the hero product is not clearly identifiable
  • Don't reuse the exact same banner crop two sends in a row — even a new angle won't overcome visual déjà vu

📦 Product Block Images

✓ Do
  • Consistent orientation per brand per send: all portrait or all landscape in one send (never mixed)
  • Product-on-white or flat-lay for product rows — lifestyle photos belong in the banner, not in product cells
  • Show the specific feature mentioned in the pain→relief copy (e.g., if copy says "seamless edge," show a close-up of the edge)
  • Min 600×600px per product image — 2-up layout at 282px display size, so source needs 2× resolution
  • File names equal the product slug — maintains linkage to UTM parameters
✗ Don't
  • No competitor products in frame — not even partially visible
  • No price overlay or watermarks on product images — price is stated in the copy block, not on the photo
  • Don't use low-res images — pixelated product shots are the fastest way to lose high-AOV segment trust
  • Don't mix product photography styles within one send (some lifestyle, some studio) — inconsistency reads as low-quality
